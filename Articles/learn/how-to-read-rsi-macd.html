How to Read RSI and MACD: Technical Analysis for Beginners

⏱ 9 min read · Last updated July 2026 · Try Fintiq Free

What is Technical Analysis?

Technical analysis is the study of a security's price and volume history to identify patterns, momentum, and probabilities about future price movements. It is not about predicting the future with certainty — no method can do that — but about identifying conditions where the probability of a particular outcome is meaningfully higher than average.

Technical analysis operates on a core premise: that the collective behaviour of all market participants — institutions, retail investors, algorithms, market makers — is reflected in the price chart. Everything that is known about a company and its prospects is, at any moment, priced in. What the chart shows you is not what the company is "worth" in a fundamental sense, but rather where the crowd thinks the price is going.

This is why technical and fundamental analysis are complementary rather than competing. Fundamental analysis asks: "What is this company worth?" Technical analysis asks: "Where is the price likely to go in the near to medium term, and what does the momentum suggest about the strength of the current trend?" Many of the most successful investors in history use both — fundamental analysis to identify what to buy, technical analysis to identify when to buy it.

RSI — The Relative Strength Index

Origin and Purpose

The Relative Strength Index was developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. and first published in his landmark 1978 book, New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems. Wilder was a mechanical engineer turned commodities trader, and he designed RSI to solve a specific problem: how to objectively measure whether a market has moved too far, too fast, in either direction — a condition traders call "overbought" or "oversold."

In the nearly five decades since its publication, RSI has become one of the most widely used technical indicators in the world, applied by traders across equities, currencies, commodities and derivatives on timeframes ranging from one-minute intraday charts to monthly charts covering decades of price history.

The Formula

RSI Formula: RSI = 100 − [100 ÷ (1 + RS)] Where: RS = Average Gain over N periods ÷ Average Loss over N periods Default period (N): 14 days (or 14 bars on whatever timeframe you use) Scale: 0 to 100 Overbought threshold: 70 Oversold threshold: 30

The formula calculates the ratio of average gains to average losses over the past 14 periods. If the market has risen in 10 of the last 14 days with large gains and fallen in only 4 with small losses, the RS value is high and RSI will be above 70. If the opposite is true — more falling days with larger moves — RSI will be below 30.

Reading RSI: Overbought and Oversold

RSI above 70 — Overbought: The stock has risen strongly and quickly relative to recent history. Selling pressure may be building. However — and this is critical — "overbought" does not mean "sell now." In a strong bull trend, RSI can remain above 70 for weeks or even months. Overbought is a caution flag, not a sell signal.

RSI below 30 — Oversold: The stock has fallen sharply and quickly. Buying interest may begin to emerge. Again, "oversold" does not mean "buy now." In a persistent bear trend, RSI can remain below 30 for extended periods as selling continues.

The most common beginner mistake with RSI is treating these levels as automatic buy or sell signals. They are not. They are alerts that draw your attention to a stock where conditions may be becoming extreme — and where additional signals (like MACD confirmation or a trend-line break) are worth watching for.

UK Example — Rolls-Royce Holdings (RR.) in 2022–2023: In early 2023, Rolls-Royce shares had endured a prolonged period of underperformance, and RSI fell to the high 20s — deeply oversold territory. For investors who were watching and had done the fundamental work to understand the business turnaround underway, this technical signal confirmed the timing. In the year that followed, Rolls-Royce was the best-performing stock in the FTSE 100, gaining over 200%. The RSI did not cause the rally — the business improvement did — but it indicated extreme selling pressure that had pushed the stock to a technically stretched low.

RSI Divergence: The Most Powerful Signal

The most valuable and reliable RSI signal is not the absolute level (above 70 or below 30), but divergence between the price chart and the RSI reading. Divergence occurs when price and momentum move in opposite directions, suggesting that the current trend is weakening.

Bullish Divergence: The price makes a new lower low (continues falling), but RSI makes a higher low (doesn't follow price down as far). This means that even though the price fell further, the selling momentum behind that fall was weaker. The market may be running out of sellers. This is often a precursor to a reversal upward.

Bearish Divergence: The price makes a new higher high (continues rising), but RSI makes a lower high (doesn't follow price up as strongly). This means the buying momentum behind the new high was weaker than the previous one. The market may be running out of buyers. This is often a precursor to a reversal downward.

Divergence signals are most powerful when they occur at significant support or resistance levels, and when confirmed by other indicators like MACD.

MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence

Origin and Purpose

MACD was developed by Gerald Appel in the late 1970s. While RSI measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes, MACD measures the relationship between two exponential moving averages (EMAs) of price — capturing the difference between a short-term trend and a longer-term trend. When these two trend lines converge or diverge, MACD signals a change in momentum.

The Components

MACD Components: MACD Line = 12-period EMA of price − 26-period EMA of price Signal Line = 9-period EMA of the MACD Line Histogram = MACD Line − Signal Line Standard settings: 12, 26, 9 (used almost universally)

The MACD Line (also called the "fast" line) measures the gap between short-term and long-term momentum. When it is positive, short-term momentum is stronger than long-term — a bullish sign. When negative, long-term momentum dominates — a bearish sign.

The Signal Line is a smoothed average of the MACD Line itself, acting as a trigger line. Crossovers between the MACD Line and the Signal Line generate buy and sell signals.

The Histogram displays the gap between the MACD Line and the Signal Line as bars above or below a zero axis. Growing bars show accelerating momentum; shrinking bars show fading momentum. Watching the histogram shrink before a crossover is one of the earliest warnings of a momentum shift.

Reading MACD: The Key Signals

Bullish Crossover: The MACD Line crosses above the Signal Line. This suggests that short-term momentum is beginning to outpace its own smoothed average — a signal that buying pressure is strengthening. This is generally interpreted as a bullish signal.

Bearish Crossover: The MACD Line crosses below the Signal Line. Selling pressure is strengthening relative to the smoothed trend. Generally interpreted as a bearish signal.

Zero Line Cross: When the MACD Line crosses above zero, it means the 12-period EMA has crossed above the 26-period EMA — the short-term trend has moved above the long-term trend. This is a bullish structural signal. A cross below zero is bearish.

Histogram Direction: Watch whether histogram bars are growing or shrinking. Bars growing taller (in either direction) mean momentum is accelerating. Bars shrinking toward zero mean momentum is fading — often an early warning of an imminent crossover.

MACD Divergence

Like RSI, MACD can exhibit divergence between price and the indicator — and this is often the most powerful MACD signal of all. Bearish MACD divergence occurs when price makes a new high but the MACD peaks at a lower level than the previous peak. This shows that even as price pushed higher, the underlying momentum behind that move was weaker — a warning that the rally is running out of conviction. Bullish MACD divergence is the mirror image.

When MACD divergence and RSI divergence occur simultaneously — both indicators showing weakening momentum at the same time — the combined signal is significantly more powerful than either alone.

Combining RSI and MACD for Stronger Signals

Using RSI and MACD together, rather than in isolation, dramatically improves signal quality. The principle is confluence: when two or more independent indicators point to the same conclusion simultaneously, the probability that the signal is genuine increases substantially.

High-Probability Bullish Setup

  • RSI is in oversold territory (below 35) and showing bullish divergence
  • MACD Line is below zero but rising, and a bullish crossover of the Signal Line has just occurred or is imminent
  • Price is at or near a significant support level (prior lows, long-term moving average)
  • The stock's long-term trend (200-day moving average) is upward

When all four conditions align, the probability of a meaningful upward move is significantly higher than any single signal in isolation.

High-Probability Bearish Setup

  • RSI is in overbought territory (above 65) and showing bearish divergence
  • MACD Line is above zero but falling, and a bearish crossover of the Signal Line has just occurred or is imminent
  • Price is at or near a significant resistance level (prior highs, extended distance from moving averages)
  • The broader market or sector is under pressure

When Signals Conflict — Wait

If RSI suggests oversold (potential buy) but MACD is in a strong downtrend with no sign of a crossover — the signals conflict. The right response is to wait. Acting on conflicting signals is one of the most common ways that technically-oriented traders take unnecessary losses. Patience until confluence emerges is a discipline that separates consistent technical traders from undisciplined ones.

The 200-Day Moving Average: Your Third Check

Adding a third dimension — the direction of the long-term trend — dramatically improves the reliability of RSI and MACD signals. The 200-day moving average (200-DMA) is the most widely watched long-term trend indicator used by institutions and professionals worldwide.

The rule is simple: trade with the long-term trend, not against it.

  • If price is above the 200-DMA, the long-term trend is up. Take bullish RSI/MACD signals seriously. Be sceptical of bearish signals — they may only represent short-term pullbacks within the uptrend.
  • If price is below the 200-DMA, the long-term trend is down. Be cautious about bullish RSI/MACD signals — they may only represent temporary bounces within a downtrend. Short-selling signals are more reliable in this environment.

This three-layer framework — RSI + MACD + 200-DMA trend direction — is the technical analysis toolkit used by many professional short-to-medium-term equity traders. It is simple enough to apply consistently but sophisticated enough to filter out the majority of false signals.

Common Mistakes Beginners Make with RSI and MACD

  1. Treating RSI above 70 as an automatic sell signal. In a strong uptrend, RSI can stay overbought for weeks. Selling every time RSI hits 70 in a bull market is a reliable way to miss large gains.
  2. Ignoring the trend direction. Taking bullish RSI signals when the stock is in a clear downtrend is fighting the dominant force in the market. Always establish trend direction first.
  3. Acting on MACD crossovers in choppy, sideways markets. MACD performs best in trending markets. In sideways conditions, it generates frequent crossovers in both directions — the classic "whipsaw" that produces many small losses.
  4. Using default settings without understanding them. The standard 14-period RSI and 12/26/9 MACD settings are reasonable starting points. But different timeframes and markets may respond better to adjusted settings. Understand what the numbers mean before changing them.
  5. Using technical analysis in isolation from fundamentals. A technically perfect bullish setup in a company that is genuinely deteriorating fundamentally will often fail. Technical signals work best when they align with a fundamentally sound business.
  6. Treating indicators as certainties rather than probabilities. No indicator is right 100% of the time. The goal is to identify setups where the probability of a particular outcome is above average — not to find a mechanical rule that always works.
